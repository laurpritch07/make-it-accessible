Color and Contrast

What WCAG says about color use:

Color is not used as the only visual means of conveying information, indicating an action, prompting a response, or distinguishing a visual element. (Level A)

WCAG SC 1.4.1

Why is color use important to accessibility?

Everyone perceives colors differently. So you shouldn't rely on color alone to communicate information.

How do I ensure that I use color appropriately?

  • Don’t convey meaning through color alone. "Incorporate alternate means of communication—use labels, texture, shapes, and patterns—to prevent confusion among people with color blindness." (Level Access 2023) Source
  • Avoid using the same color to mean different things. "Use color consistently throughout your interface, especially when you use it to help communicate information like status or interactivity." (Apple 2024) Source
  • Avoid certain color combinations. "There are certain color combinations that are especially bad for users who are color blind. Red/green color blindness is the most common, so avoid green on red or red on green. (Think especially about avoiding using red and green for “bad” and “good” indicators)." (Carnegie Museums of Pittsburgh, n.d.) Source
  • Use texture and patterns. "When you need to emphasize certain objects, it’s a good idea to incorporate textures and/or patterns. This is especially helpful for charts and graphs where color is often used to differentiate between items. Adding textures and patterns like checkers, lines, and dots can help make each item stand out as unique and easily identifiable." (Rank 2023) Source
  • Test it. Some users have difficulty perceiving color, testing your color combinations by using a tool like Whocanuse.com will help you understand how color contrast affects people with different visual impairments.

What WCAG says about color contrast:

The visual presentation of text and images of text has a contrast ratio of at least 4.5:1, except for the following:

  • Large Text. Large-scale text and images of large-scale text have a contrast ratio of at least 3:1;
  • Incidental. Text or images of text that are part of an inactive user interface component, that are pure decoration, that are not visible to anyone, or that are part of a picture that contains significant other visual content, have no contrast requirement.
  • Logotypes. Text that is part of a logo or brand name has no contrast. requirement. (Level AA)

WCAG SC 1.4.3

Why is contrast important to accessibility?

"People with low vision often have difficulty reading text that does not contrast with its background. This can be exacerbated if the person has a color vision deficiency that lowers the contrast even further. Providing a minimum luminance contrast ratio between the text and its background can make the text more readable even if the person does not see the full range of colors. It also works for the rare individuals who see no color." (W3C Web Accessibility Initiative (WAI)) Source

How do I ensure that I use contrast appropriately?

  • Aim for a middle ground. "The issue with color contrast can be complicated as some people with cognitive disabilities are sensitive to light and could experience eye fatigue or feel distracted when looking at high contrast text and images, such as black text on a white background at a 21:1 contrast ratio. A good middle ground is to aim for a 7:1 contrast ratio, the AAA rating for WCAG." (Zaraysky 2024) Source
  • Convert it to grayscale. Use a non-destructive FIGMA plugin like Grayscale to convert your designs to grayscale. This will help you understand if any design elements are indistinguishable or difficult to read due to poor color contrast.
  • Test it. Use WebAim’s Link Contrast Checker to ensure your links have at least 3:1 contrast with surrounding body text and a 4.5:1 contrast with the background.

What WCAG says about non-text contrast:

The visual presentation of the following have a contrast ratio of at least 3:1 against adjacent color(s):

  • User Interface Components. Visual information required to identify user interface components and states, except for inactive components or where the appearance of the component is determined by the user agent and not modified by the author;
  • Graphical Objects Parts of graphics required to understand the content, except when a particular presentation of graphics is essential to the information being conveyed. (Level AA)

WCAG SC 1.4.11

Why is non-text contrast important to accessibility?

The contrast of graphics is just as important as that of text. If an icon or graphical object is needed to understand the content or functionality of a webpage then it should have a minimum contrast ration of 3:1.

How do I ensure that graphics are perceivable?

  • Consider adjacent colors. If color is required to understand a control’s relationship to other controls (i.e., which radio button is selected and which ones aren’t), you also need to ensure that there is a 3:1 contrast between any colors used to indicate those differences. For example, the center color of a selected radio button must have a 3:1 color ratio to the center color of an unselected radio button. (Miller 2022) Source
  • Use sufficent contrast to indicate state changes. Each state of the component must also have 3:1 contrast. States are temporary changes in a component, usually because of a user interaction, such as hovering with a mouse or tabbing with a keyboard. (WebAIM 2024) Source
  • Test it. Use WebAim’s Link Contrast Checker to ensure your links have at least 3:1 contrast with surrounding body text and a 4.5:1 contrast with the background.

Typography and Language

What WCAG says about choosing a typeface:

WCAG doesn't provide any guidance related to font selection. However, there are a few best practices outlined below that will enhance readability.

Why is choosing an appropriate typeface important?

"Accessible typography ensures that textual information is accessible to all users, irrespective of their abilities or disabilities." (Section508 2024) Source

How do I consider accessibility when choosing a typeface?

  • "Select clear and readable typefaces. A distinct and straightforward typeface greatly enhances readability. Avoid character ambiguity, where two characters appear remarkably alike, to avoid confusion (like the capital “I” and lowercase “L” ). The more straightforward the typeface, the easier it will be for your users to read."" (Lange 2024) Source
  • Choose typefaces with a large x-height. "A large x-height enhances legibility by creating more space for lowercase letterforms." (Brewer 2016) Source
  • Avoid imposter letter shapes. "Some typefaces have letterform designs that are virtually identical for multiple letters. The letters “I1l” (upper case ‘i’, the number one and lower case L in Gill Sans) are a good example of this issue. To avoid this problem choose a typeface or font with distinct features on both the top and/or the bottom of the capital ‘I’ and a short but noticeable arm on the top of the number ‘1’. (Williams 2020) Source
  • Limit font styles and varieties. "Limit font styles and varieties. Numerous typefaces or font styles can disorient users. Maintain a small, consistent set of fonts to aid readability and reduce cognitive load, offering a more consistent user interface." (Lange 2024) Source

What WCAG says about text size:

Except for captions and images of text, text can be resized without assistive technology up to 200 percent without loss of content or functionality.

WCAG SC 1.4.4

Why is desiging for resize important?

By designing for text to be resized, people with low vision will be able to easily view content by increasing text size so that they can read content without compromise.

How do I design for text to resize?

  • Design for reflow. “Design to reflow to a minimum viewport size of 320 CSS pixels wide, and 256 pixels high. At this size, there must be no loss of content or functionality, and scrolling must not be required in more than one direction. (Rallo et al. 53) Source
  • 80 characters or less. “Use relative measurements to set column widths so that lines can average 80 characters or less when the browser is resized. This makes it possible for users with certain reading or vision disabilities that have trouble keeping their place when reading long lines of text to view and interact with the content more efficiently. (W3C Web Accessibility Initiative (WAI)) Source
  • Make sure your text isn't too small. “The exact dimensions of font sizes can vary depending on the unit used, the size of the viewport (if units are relative), and the pixel density of the device’s screen, but a safe bet would be to use 16px or 1em or 1rem as the smallest size for body type.” (Zaraysky 2024) Source
  • Test it. Check to see that line length can be set to 80 characters or less by resizing the browser window.

What WCAG says about spacing:

In content implemented using markup languages that support the following text style properties, no loss of content or functionality occurs by setting all of the following and by changing no other style property:

  • Line height (line spacing) to at least 1.5 times the font size;
  • Spacing following paragraphs to at least 2 times the font size;
  • Letter spacing (tracking) to at least 0.12 times the font size;
  • Word spacing to at least 0.16 times the font size.

WCAG SC 1.4.12

Why is text spacing important to accessibility?

Some people need text with different spacing. Ensure users can adjust text by designing content in a way that adapts to their needs.

How do I design for text spacing?

  • Use scalable rather than absolute units. "Mobile devices and browsers include features to allow users to adjust their font size system-wide. Be sure to not hard set the line heights when coding text as dynamic and adjustable. Otherwise, a reader may choose a large text size but be unable to read it because the lines of content overlap." (Zaraysky 2024.) Source
  • Maintain clarity in letter and word spacing. “Avoid crowding letters and tightly packed text, which can be tough, especially for users with reading difficulties. Maintain appropriate spacing using CSS properties like ‘letter-spacing’ and ‘word-spacing’ to ensure clarity.” (Lange 2024) Source
  • Line Spacing. “Too little leading will overly emphasize word-spacing and in effect reduce readability. Too much leading will give readers difficulty in tracking from one line of text to the next, and may also increase strain on the neck. Aim for a comfortable balance, and if in doubt, err on the side of slightly more leading. (Rallo et al. 29) Source
  • Paragraph spacing. "Increase the amount of space after each paragraph to be noticeably greater than the line spacing. Aim for a minimum of 50% more space between paragraphs than between lines." (Rallo et al. 29) Source

What WCAG says about headings and labels:

Headings and labels describe topic or purpose. (Level AA)

WCAG SC 2.4.6

Why are descriptive headings and labels important to accessibility?

"Descriptive headings are especially helpful for users who have disabilities that make reading slow and for people with limited short-term memory. These people benefit when section titles make it possible to predict what each section contains." (W3 2024) Source

How do I write clear and descriptive headings?

  • Make a plan.“It is best to plan out a heading structure before composing a page. Doing so will help you both select appropriate heading levels and keep your thoughts organized overall.” (Yale University 2024) Source
  • Organize the content into sections. "Divide the page into main sections and begin each section with a descriptive heading. Then add subheadings where appropriate. A section may consist of one, two or more paragraphs; however, make sure you don’t add too many subheadings because this may make your content harder to read." (Pearson 2024) Source
  • Put the most important information first. “Authors may also want to consider putting the most important information at the beginning of each heading. This helps users “skim" the headings to locate the specific content they need, and is especially helpful when browsers or assistive technology allow navigation from heading to heading.” (W3) Source
  • Make it scannable. “Use short copy and bulleted lists rather than big blocks of text. This is easier for all readers to scan and it improves accessibility for users who depend on screen readers.” (Yale University 2024) Source
  • Test it. Use axe for Designers FIGMA plugin to run checks on your design files to ensure headings are appropriately structured.

What WCAG says about Non-Text Content:

All non-text content that is presented to the user has a text alternative that serves the equivalent purpose, except for certain situations. (Level A)

WCAG SC 1.1.1

Why is including text alternatives important?

"Providing text alternatives allows the information to be rendered in a variety of ways by a variety of user agents. For example, a person who cannot see a picture can have the text alternative read aloud using synthesized speech. A person who cannot hear an audio file can have the text alternative displayed so that he or she can read it." (W3C Web Accessibility Initiative (WAI)) Source

How do I write accessible alt-text?

  • Images and pictures. “Make sure to convey the content and the purpose of an image in a concise and unambiguous manner. The alt text shouldn’t be longer than a sentence or two—most of the time a few thoughtfully selected words will do. Consider what is important about an image. For example, important context might be the setting, the emotions on people's faces, the colors, or the relative sizes.” (Microsoft 2024) Source
  • Avoid redundant prefixes. “Usually, there’s no need to include words like “image”, “icon”, or “picture” in the alt text. People who can see will know this already, and screen readers announce the presence of an image. In some situations, it may be important to distinguish between paintings, photographs, or illustrations, etc., but it’s best to avoid the more generic use of the terms.” (W3C Web Accessibility Initiative (WAI)) Source
  • Logos."Logos are never decorative, so they require alt text. Describe any significant symbols or graphics, and include any text in the logo word for word in the alt text." (Section508.gov) Source
  • Test it. Include alt-text in your design files and check it by using a plugin like Stark Alt-Text Annotations This plugin will help ensure you include alt-text for all visual content as well as check for common errors like redundant prefixes (“picture of”).

What WCAG says about images of text:

If the technologies being used can achieve the visual presentation, text is used to convey information rather than images of text except for the following:

  • Customizable. The image of text can be visually customized to the user's requirements;
  • Essential. A particular presentation of text is essential to the information being conveyed.
  • Note. Logotypes (text that is part of a logo or brand name) are considered essential. (Level AA)

WCAG SC 1.4.5

Why is it important to use text to convey information rather than images?

Users need to be able to alter text to meet their personal accessibility needs and they can't do that if the text is inside an image.

How?

  • Use real text instead of pictures of text. With the current CSS capabilities in most web browsers, it is good design practice to use actual text that is styled with CSS rather than image-based text presentation. Genuine text is much more flexible than images: It can be resized without losing clarity, and background and text colors can be modified to suit the reading preferences of users.(W3C Web Accessibility Initiative (WAI)) Source
  • If the image of text is absolutely necessary (e.g., logos). Always provide alternative text for words in a picture meant to be read. Upload vector images (SVG) or images with enough extra resolution to stay sharp when enlarged (e.g., a 1000px wide file for a 500px wide placement) Source

What WCAG says about link purpose:

The purpose of each link can be determined from the link text alone or from the link text together with its programmatically determined link context, except where the purpose of the link would be ambiguous to users in general.

WCAG SC 2.4.4

Why is link purpose important to accessibility?

Descriptive link titles help users predict where they are headed. This is especially important for people with visual and cognitive disabilities.

How do I ensure that link text is accessible?

  • Make it obvious.“Like headings, link text should be autonomously meaningful.” (Pickering 92) Source
  • Put the most important information first.Place the distinguishing information of links at the beginning of a link. Don't put extra information first, For example, don't say "Link opens in a new window: Products." Instead, say "Products (opens in a new window)" (or something along those lines). This is especially important in this example if several links open in a new window. With the explanatory information first instead of the main information, screen reader users would have to listen to the phrase "link opens in a new window" over and over again. They will have a harder time distinguishing between different links, or at least it will take them longer. Source
  • Test it. Review your content for links and confirm that the text in each link has a autonomously meaningful and concise, revise if necessary.

Layout and Hierarchy

What WCAG says about meaningful sequence:

When the sequence in which content is presented affects its meaning, a correct reading sequence can be programmatically determined. (Level A)

WCAG SC 1.3.2

Why is meaningful sequence important for accessibility?

If a user is using a screen reader or has css disabled, information can be coded to ensure content is presented to the user in the proper order.

How do I ensure pages have meaningful content order?

  • Use a familiar hierarchy and design. “When designing web pages, select standard components that look and behave the way users expect. Use standard conventions for layout such as:home link in the upper left corner, navigation at the top, and search in the upper right.” (W3C Web Accessibility Initiative (WAI)) Source
  • Navigation should be logical and intuitive. “Be careful when changing the visual position of elements on screen using CSS. This can cause the tab order to jump around, seemingly at random, confusing users who rely on the keyboard." (Nash et al.) Source
  • Indicate reading order in your design files. If the visual presentation affects the sequencing, remember to communicate the appropriate sequence to development. A plugin like A11y - Focus Order by Microsoft Design can help you annotate the focus/tab order in your designs.

What WCAG says about orientation:

Content does not restrict its view and operation to a single display orientation, such as portrait or landscape, unless a specific display orientation is essential. (Level AA)

WCAG SC 1.3.4

Why is orientation important to accessibility?

"Some users have their devices mounted in a fixed orientation (e.g. on the arm of a power wheelchair). Therefore, websites and applications need to support both orientations by not restricting the orientation." Users with dexterity impairments, who have a mounted device will be able to use the content in their fixed orientation. Users with low-vision will be able to view content in the orientation that works best for them, for example to increase the text size by viewing content in landscape." (W3C Web Accessibility Initiative (WAI))

How can I design content to be responsive?

  • Design content to reflow. “The visual presentation shouldn’t require any specific screen size, orientation, device, browser, or any other limitations on the part of the user. Instead, the design and content should gracefully reflow and wrap from one viewport to another, regardless of the size or zoom level. This is sometimes referred to as responsive or liquid design, and this flexibility is at the core of an accessible visual presentation.” (Rallo et al. 53) Source
  • Test it. Preview your design on multiple devices, using different orientations, and text sizes. Start by testing on the smallest and largest layouts. Source

Interactions and Media

What WCAG says about target size:

The size of the target for pointer inputs is at least 24 by 24 CSS pixels, with some exceptions.(Level AA)

WCAG SC 2.5.8

Why are target sizes important in designing for accessibility?

"Having targets with sufficient size - or at least sufficient target spacing - can help all users who may have difficulty in confidently targeting or operating small controls." (W3C Web Accessibility Initiative (WAI))

How can I design accessible targets?

  • Make custom targets at least 48 pixels. “A minimum recommended touch target size is around 48 device independent pixels on a site with a properly set mobile viewport. For example, while an icon may only have a width and height of 24px, you can use additional padding to bring the tap target size up to 48px. The 48x48 pixel area corresponds to around 9mm, which is about the size of a person's finger pad area.” (Gash et al.) Source
  • Space targets about 8 pixels apart.“Touch targets should also be spaced about 8 pixels apart, both horizontally and vertically, so that a user's finger pressing on one tap target does not inadvertently touch another tap target.” (Gash et al.) Source
  • Frequency of Touches. Consider making targets that are repeatedly or frequently pressed larger than the minimum size. (Microsoft). Source
  • Test it. Ensure that your touch targets are accessible by using a Figma plugin like Stark Contrast & Accessibility Checker to inspect your designs.

What WCAG says about focus indicators?

Any keyboard operable user interface has a mode of operation where the keyboard focus indicator is visible. (Level AA)

WCAG SC 2.4.7

Why is focus visible important for accessibility?

Focus indicators help sighted keyboard users to operate the page.

How can I design accessible focus indicators?

  • Make a list of all focusable elements to design for. “Links, buttons, form fields, menu items, things triggered by hover (like tooltips), Widgets (like a calendar picker)” (Pasi) Source
  • Improve but don’t remove. “Browsers implement their own focus styles ranging from dotted outlines to fuzzy blue halos. You can replace these default styles, but you should never just remove them.” (Pickering 61) Source
  • When in doubt, use the hover state. “In a lot of cases, a good focus state might be the same as the hover state. If your hover state is obvious, consider using that as a focus indication as well.” (Pasi) Source
  • Test it. Does the focus indicator have sufficient contrast, is it consistent across browsers?

What WCAG says about Audio control:

If any audio on a Web page plays automatically for more than 3 seconds, either a mechanism is available to pause or stop the audio, or a mechanism is available to control audio volume independently from the overall system volume level. (Level A)

WCAG SC 1.4.2

Why is audio control important for accessiblity?

"Individuals who use screen reading software can find it hard to hear the speech output if there is other audio playing at the same time. This difficulty is exacerbated when the screen reader's speech output is software based (as most are today) and is controlled via the same volume control as the sound. Therefore, it is important that the user be able to turn off the background sound.” (W3C Web Accessibility Initiative (WAI))

How can I ensure audio is sensitive to a users needs?

  • Don't play any audio content automatically.
  • Ensure that audio control is easy to locate."If you do have audio that automatically plays, limit it to three seconds and provide an easy way to adjust, mute, pause, or stop the video." Source
